This guide highlights important differences from High German to Zurich Swiss German (which we'll just refer to as "Swiss German" from now on, though it's only one of many widely varying dialects). It aspires to represent as many of these as possible in a set of N examples. To resolve ambiguity, the examples are longer than in typical vocabulary lists. Most entries include both English and High German translations. The translations should be unambiguously valid from Swiss German to the other languages: they may not be in the other direction.

The guide assumes English fluency and High German understanding at the level of hoped-for Swiss German understanding.

You can guess much of Swiss German based on High German. People who speak neither language usually start with High German; it's the written and official language for Swiss German speakers, and is easier to teach (and certify) in a formal setting. This doesn't imply that High German is higher status or more useful in daily Swiss life, though.

Swiss German is a spoken language, so this text-only format is severely lacking; you should have some way to listen to the language. A logical improvement for this page would be to include audio clips for the Swiss German terms. There is no standardized way to write Swiss German (and no real standardization at all for Swiss German, even within individual Swiss German dialects like Zurich Swiss German).

The rest of this document motivates the choice of examples. You can find a TSV file of all the examples here, which you can import for practice into a tool like Anki.

Entries focus first on examples of common ways to map Swiss German to High German, then move on to commonly used grammar and vocabulary which differ in other ways from High German. The level is roughly A1.

We'll present examples here as

  • Swiss German term

Mapping High German to Swiss German

There are several common patterns between corresponding words in High German and Swiss German. Not all of these appear well in writing.

Swiss German words are often missing an "n" at the end, compared to the corresponding High German word:

  • scho
  • vo
  • de Maa
  • mache
  • eifach

In some cases this isn't even consistently done, even within Zurich Swiss German, particularly with some first-person verb conjugations:

  • ich bin
  • ich chan

You often also lose "ch".

  • au
  • no
  • mit oi
  • dur
  • Züri
  • äntli
  • welles

Other letters can drop off the end as well:

  • grad
  • jetz
  • gääl

You will also notice a lot more hard "ch" sounds (Like Scottish "loch"). When "ch" or "k" appears in a High German word, the corresponding Swiss German word almost always has a hard "ch", though it's often still written as it is in High German.

  • kai
  • koche

If you do want a "k" sound as in English, this becomes "gg" when written

  • d Brugg
  • zrugg

"st" often becomes "scht" (not just at the beginning of words)

  • de Schtärn
  • geschter
  • du bisch
  • er isch

"sp" similarly often becomes "schp".

  • schpile
  • de Gascht

"b" and "p" are closer together, as are "t" and "d", so often these change relative to High German, when written.

  • de Puure
  • Dütsch
  • dörfe

Vowels often shift:

"i" often becomes "ie", with two vowels sounds (a dipthong)

  • d Liecht

Many words already written with "ie" in High German are also pronounced this way

  • Wie bitte?
  • de Spiegel

Sometimes "ie" becomes "üü", though!

  • tüüf

"e" often becomes "aa" or "ä"(which doesn't sound exactly like High German "ä")

  • Wie gaats?
  • schlächt

"ä" can sound different

  • de Chäfer

"i" can become "u"

  • d Brüle

"u" often becomes the two-syllable "uä" or "ue"

  • s Buäch
  • guet

"au" often becomes "uu" (or "üü")

  • d Muus
  • s Huus
  • bruuche

"eu" often becomes or "ü" or "üü"

  • hütt
  • Lüüt
  • d Füürzüüg

"ei" often becomes "ii" or "i" (a long vowel sound)

  • sii
  • d Ziit
  • bi ois

"o" can become "u"

  • d Wuche
  • d Sunne

The diminuitives "-lein" or "-chen" usually become "-li". As in High German, these are always neuter.

  • s Brötli

The "-ig" ending usually sounds like "-ik" in English, even when it would sound like a soft "-ich" ending in High Geman. The "-ung" ending in High German often becomes "-ig" in Swiss German.

  • d Quittig
  • d Rächnig

Many common prepositions change in a predictable way

  • uufruume
  • iistiige
  • uusstiige
  • aafange

Those also apply when used as verb prefixes. Other prefixes are commonly shortened by dropping the vowel

  • grad
  • zrugg
  • bsuäche
  • ich ha gmacht

Place names also change in similar ways

  • Züri
  • Schtai am Rhii

Articles

Swiss German uses the same three genders as High German, and the nominative, accusative, and dative cases (not the genitive). The accusative form of words is the same as the nominative, outside of a few pronouns.

Swiss German neuter articles and possessives sometimes feature an "s" on the end, like High German adjectives sometimes do.

The definite articles in the nominative and accusative look like abbreviated versions of the High German nominative ones:

  • de Maa
  • d Frau
  • s Chind
  • d Chind
The indefinite articles in the nominative differ more.
  • en Maa
  • e Frau
  • es Chind
  • Chind
As in High German, you add a "k" (but pronounced as a hard "ch") to the front to negate these. Here the pronunciation also changes back to something closer to High German.
  • kain Maa
  • kai Frau
  • kais Chind
  • kai Chind
The definite articles in the dative also look like the High German ones, with either the first or last letter chopped off
  • mit em Maa
  • mit de Frau
  • mit em Chind
  • mit de Chind
The indefinite, dative articles
  • mit emene Maa
  • mit enere Frau
  • mit emene Chind
  • mit Chind

Inversions

In situations, like questions, where the subject follows the verb, the verb and subject often combine into a single, combined form. This happens often in informal, spoken English, for instance "gimme" or "giv'em" instead of "give me" or "give them", respectively. The inverted form of "du" can just be "du", but is often nothing, so Swiss German is "pro drop" in this sense. The "ich" and especially "mir" (we) forms can sound quite distinct from the two-word inversions you'd expect from High German.

Nominative:

  • Hani Ziit?
  • Häsch Hunger?
  • Hatr Hunge?
  • Hatsi Hunger?
  • Wämmer go skifahre?
  • Gömmer go poschte?
  • Hammer öppis gha?
  • Simmer verspötet?
  • Macheder Spass?

Inversions are also common with reflexive pronouns. This includes the both the accusative (note that mich and dich drop the 'ch' when inverted)

  • Du froischdi.
  • Ich rasiärmi hütt nöd.
  • Ich wäschmer hütt d Haar nöd.

and the dative.

  • Wiä oft putzischder d Zää?.

New Grammar and Constructions

Swiss German has a few points of grammar and constructions that don't appear in High German.

go

When coming or going in order to do something (a verb), add "go" before the verb. This can look superficially like English sentences like "I'm going to go work", but this comparison is more confusing than helpful.
  • Ich gang go schaffe.
  • Ich chume go schlafe.

Relative pronouns

You use "wo" for all relative pronouns in Swiss German.
  • Ich bi de Maa, wo da wont.

Tuä + verb

One can use "tuä" as a (virtually meaningless?) auxiliary verb.
  • Tuänd Si tschuute?
  • Ich tuä gärn tanze.

n-liasons

We noted that Swiss German tends to drop "n"s, compared to High German. This leaves a lot of vowels at the ends of words, so often an "n" will be introduced to make things flow better. (This also commonly appears in the "ich" inverted verb forms below).
  • Guete-n-aabig.
  • Min Name-n-isch Tobler.

Verbs

The standard conjugation only has four forms. All the plural forms are the same!
  • mache
  • ich mache
  • du machsch
  • er macht
  • mir mached

Swiss German only uses the compound past tense - there is no preterit (not even "ich war"). Interestingly "gsi" is more regular than the High German "gewesen".

  • ich ha gmacht
  • ich bin gsi

Past participles work fairly similarly to High German, except that verbs starting with g-, k- ,ch-, b- ,p-, d- ,t-, or z- don't have the "g-" prefix you might expect in the past particple.

  • ich bin cho
  • ich bin gange
