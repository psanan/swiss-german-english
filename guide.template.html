Goal: Starting from some knowledge of High German (and English), understand as much Zurich Swiss German as possible with a focused set of N examples.

We'll highlight important differences from High German, purely by example. The examples are longer than in typical vocabulary lists, to resolve ambiguity. Most entries will include both English and High German translations. The translations should be unambiguously valid from Swiss German to the other languages: they may not be in the other direction.

This assumes you understand High German at least as well as you hope to understand Swiss German. You can guess much of Swiss German based on High German. People who speak neither language often choose to learn High German before Swiss German; it's the written and official language for Swiss German speakers, and there are formalized systems to learn it and be certified (as immigrants like me must do to gain residence permits). This doesn't imply that High German is higher status or more useful in daily Swiss life, though: it's a cliché that Swiss people would often rather speak English than High German. Thus, being able to listen to Swiss German is important, even if one speaks in High German.

You should also have some way to listen to Swiss German. It's a spoken language, so this text-only format is severely lacking on its own. Hopefully it helps to have a foreigner like me try to describe the language from experience trying to learn it, but you also need to be around people speaking it and to be able to connect it to events in your real life. Mapping High German to Swiss German

There are several common patterns between corresponding words in High German and Swiss German. Not all of these appear well in writing.

"N"s at the end of words in High German commonly disappear.

  • scho
  • vo
  • de Maa

In some cases this isn't even consistently done, even within Zurich Swiss German, particularly with some first-person verb conjugations:

  • ich bin
  • ich chan

You often also lose "ch".

  • au
  • no
  • mit oi
  • dur
  • Züri
  • äntli

Other letters can drop off the end as well

  • grad
  • jetz

And prefixes often lose an "e"
  • grad
  • graad ? (TODO)
You will also notice a lot more hard "ch" sounds (Like Scottish "loch"). When "ch" or "k" appears in a High German word, the corresponding Swiss German word almost always has a hard "ch", though it's often still written as it is in High German.
  • kai
  • koche
If you do want a "k" sound as in English, this becomes "gg" when written
  • d Brugg
  • zrugg
"st" often becomes "scht"
  • geschter
  • du bisch
  • er isch
  • du hasch
  • de Schtärn
"sp" similarly often becomes "schp", even when it wouldn't be pronounced that way in High German
  • schpile
  • de Gascht
"b" and "p" are closer together, as are "t" and "d", so often these change relative to High German, when written. In general, these are unaspirated.
  • de Puure
  • Dütsch
  • dörfe
Vowels often shift: "i" often becomes "ie", with two vowels sounds (a dipthong)
  • d Liecht
Many words already written with "ie" in High German are also pronounced this way
  • wie bitte?
  • de Spiegel
Sometimes "ie" becomes "üü", though!
  • tüüf
"e" often becomes "aa" or "ä"(which doesn't sound exactly like High German "ä")
  • wie gaat's?
  • schlächt
"ä" can sound different
  • de Chäfer
"i" can become "u"
  • d Brüle
"u" often becomes the two-syllable "uä" or "ue"
  • s Buäch
  • guet
"au" often becomes "uu" (or "üü")
  • d Muus
"eu" often becomes or "ü" or "üü"
  • hütt
  • Lüüt
  • d Füürzüüg
"ei" often becomes "ii" or "i" (a long vowel sound)
  • sii
  • d Ziit
  • bi ois
"o" can become "u"
  • d Wuche
  • d Sunne
The diminuitives "-lein" or "-chen" usually become "-li". As in High German, these are always neuter.
  • s Brötli
The "-ung" ending often becomes "-ig". As in High German, "g" at the end of the word sounds more like "k" in English.
  • d Quittig
  • d Rächnig
Many common prepositions change in a predictable way
  • uufruume
  • iistiige
  • uusstiige
  • aafange
Those also apply when used as verb prefixes. Other prefixes are commonly shortened by dropping the vowel
  • zrugg
  • bsuäche
  • gmacht
Place and language names also change in similar ways
  • Züri
  • Schtai am Rhii

Articles

Swiss German uses the same three genders as High German, and the nominative, accusative, and dative cases (never the genitive). The accusative form of words is the same as the nominative, outside of a few pronouns. Unlike High German, Swiss German neuter articles and possessives sometimes feature an "s" on the end, like High German adjectives sometimes do. The definite articles in the nominative and accusative look like abbreviated versions of the High German nominative ones:
  • de Maa
  • d Frau
  • s Chind
  • d Chind
The indefinite articles in the nominative differ more.
  • en Maa
  • e Frau
  • es Chind
  • Chind
As in High German, you add a "k" (but pronounced as a hard "ch") to the front to negate these. but here the pronunciation also changes:
  • kain Maa
  • kai Frau
  • kais Chind
  • kai Chind
The definite articles in the dative also look like the High German ones, with either the first or last letter chopped off
  • mit em Maa
  • mit de Frau
  • mit em Chind
  • mit de Chind
The indefinite, dative articles
  • mit emene Maa
  • mit enere Frau
  • mit emene Chind
  • mit Chind

Pronouns

In the nominative, the only really surprising pronoun "mir" meaning "we".

  • ich bin
  • du bisch
  • er isch
  • si isch
  • es isch
  • mir sind
  • ir sind
  • Si sind

The accusative pronouns are either the same as High German or the Swiss German dative form.

  • für mich
  • für dich
  • für in/si/(e)s
  • für ois
  • für oi
  • für si/Si

The dative pronouns:

  • mit mir
  • mit dir
  • mit im/ire/im
  • mit ois
  • mit oi
  • mit ine/Ine

(See below that each of these also has an inverted form)

Adjective endings

As in High German, there are quite a few adjective ending rules to learn. However, there is no distinction between the nominative and accusative, the dative is almost uniform, and there is no genitive! Some forms can either be nothing or "-i".
  • de gross(i) Maa - the big man
  • di gross(i) Frau - the big woman
  • s alte Huus - the old house
  • di grosse Chind - the big children
  • en grosse Maa - a big man
  • e grossi Frau - a big woman
  • es alts Huus - an old house
  • grosse Maa - big man
  • grossi Frau - big woman
  • alts Huus - old house
  • grossi Chind - big children
  • min grosse Maa - my big husband
  • mini gross Frau - my big wife
  • mis alte Huus - my old house
  • mini grosse Chind - my big children
  • mit em grosse Maa - with the big man
  • mit de grosse Frau - with the big woman
  • mit em grosse Chind - with the big child
  • mit de grosse Chind - with the big children
  • mit emene grosse Maa - with a big man
  • mit enere grosse Frau - with a big woman
  • mit emene grosse Chind - with a big child
  • mit grossem Maa - with big man
  • mit grosse(r) Frau - with big woman
  • mit grossem Chind - with big child
  • mit grosse Chind - with big children
  • mit mim grosse Maa - with my big husband
  • mit minere grosse Frau - with my big wife
  • mit mim grosse Chind - with my big child
  • mit mine grosse Chind - with my big children

New Grammar and Constructions

Swiss German has a few points of grammar and constructions that don't appear in High German.

Inversions and pro-drops

TODO normal inversion examples (gommer, gahni, etc.) The inverted form of "du" can just be "du", but is often nothing, so Swiss German is "pro drop" in this sense
  • Hasch Hunger?
  • Hasch du Hunger?
In the accusative, with reflexive verbs, you have different inversions, including one for "dich"
  • Ich froimi seer
  • (something iwth -di)

go

When coming or going in order to do something (a verb), add "go" before the verb. This can look superficially like English sentences like "I'm going to go work", but this comparison is more confusing than helpful.
  • Ich gang go schaffe : I'm going (somewhere) to work
  • Ich chume go schlafe : I'm coming to sleep

Relative pronouns

You use "wo" for all relative pronouns in Swiss German.
  • Ich bi de Maa, wo da wont

Demonstrative Pronouns

  • dies (??)

Tuä + verb

One can use "tuä" as a (virtually meaningless?) auxiliary verb.
  • Tuänd Si tschuute?
  • Ich tuä gärn tanze

n-liasons

We noted that Swiss German tends to drop "n"s, compared to High German. This leaves a lot of vowels at the ends of words, so often an "n" will be introduced to make things flow better.
  • guete-n-aabig
  • Min Name-n-isch

Verbs

The standard conjugation only has four forms. All the plural forms are the same!
  • ich mache
  • du machsch
  • er macht
  • mir mached
Swiss German only uses the compound past tense, never the preterit. This includes with "sii" (to be). Compared to High German, past participles more often lack the "g-" prefix, beyond those that start with unseparable prefixes.
  • Ich bin gsii
Interestingly, that's actually more regular than high German, and "sii" follows the standard mappings above
  • sii
  • ich bin
  • du bisch
  • er isch
  • mir sind
  • ich bin gsi
