Swiss German in N flashcards

Objective: Starting from English fluency and some knowledge of High German, understand as much Zurich Swiss German as possible with a focused set of N examples.

This guide highlights important differences from High German, purely by example. The examples are longer than in typical vocabulary lists, to resolve ambiguity. Most entries will include both English and High German translations. The translations should be unambiguously valid from Swiss German to the other languages: they may not be in the other direction.

I assume you understand High German at least as well as you hope to understand Swiss German. You can guess much of Swiss German based on High German. People who speak neither language often choose to learn High German before Swiss German; it's the written and official language for Swiss German speakers, and there are formal certification processes. This doesn't imply that High German is higher status or more useful in daily Swiss life, though: it's a cliché for good reason that Swiss people would often rather speak English than High German. Thus, being able to listen to Swiss German is useful, even if one speaks in High German.

You should also have some way to listen to Swiss German. It's a spoken language, so this text-only format is severely lacking on its own. Hopefully it helps to have a foreigner like me try to describe the language from experience trying to learn it, but you also need to be around people speaking it and to be able to connect it to events in your real life. The next logical step for this project is to include audio for the Swiss German terms.

Mapping High German to Swiss German

There are several common patterns between corresponding words in High German and Swiss German. Not all of these appear well in writing.

"N"s at the end of words in High German commonly disappear.

  • scho
  • vo
  • de Maa

In some cases this isn't even consistently done, even within Zurich Swiss German, particularly with some first-person verb conjugations:

  • ich bin
  • ich chan

You often also lose "ch".

  • au
  • no
  • mit oi
  • dur
  • Züri
  • äntli

Other letters can drop off the end as well

  • grad
  • jetz
  • gääl

And prefixes often lose an "e"
  • grad
  • ich ha gmacht
You will also notice a lot more hard "ch" sounds (Like Scottish "loch"). When "ch" or "k" appears in a High German word, the corresponding Swiss German word almost always has a hard "ch", though it's often still written as it is in High German.
  • kai
  • koche
If you do want a "k" sound as in English, this becomes "gg" when written
  • d Brugg
  • zrugg
"st" often becomes "scht"
  • geschter
  • du bisch
  • er isch
  • de Schtärn
"sp" similarly often becomes "schp", even when it wouldn't be pronounced that way in High German
  • schpile
  • de Gascht
"b" and "p" are closer together, as are "t" and "d", so often these change relative to High German, when written. In general, these are unaspirated.
  • de Puure
  • Dütsch
  • dörfe
Vowels often shift: "i" often becomes "ie", with two vowels sounds (a dipthong)
  • d Liecht
Many words already written with "ie" in High German are also pronounced this way
  • Wie bitte?
  • de Spiegel
Sometimes "ie" becomes "üü", though!
  • tüüf
"e" often becomes "aa" or "ä"(which doesn't sound exactly like High German "ä")
  • Wie gaats?
  • schlächt
"ä" can sound different
  • de Chäfer
"i" can become "u"
  • d Brüle
"u" often becomes the two-syllable "uä" or "ue"
  • s Buäch
  • guet
"au" often becomes "uu" (or "üü")
  • d Muus
"eu" often becomes or "ü" or "üü"
  • hütt
  • Lüüt
  • d Füürzüüg
"ei" often becomes "ii" or "i" (a long vowel sound)
  • sii
  • d Ziit
  • bi ois
"o" can become "u"
  • d Wuche
  • d Sunne
The diminuitives "-lein" or "-chen" usually become "-li". As in High German, these are always neuter.
  • s Brötli
The "-ung" ending often becomes "-ig". As in High German, "g" at the end of the word sounds more like "k" in English.
  • d Quittig
  • d Rächnig
Many common prepositions change in a predictable way
  • uufruume
  • iistiige
  • uusstiige
  • aafange
Those also apply when used as verb prefixes. Other prefixes are commonly shortened by dropping the vowel
  • zrugg
  • bsuäche
  • ich ha gmacht
Place names also change in similar ways
  • Züri
  • Schtai am Rhii

Articles

Swiss German uses the same three genders as High German, and the nominative, accusative, and dative cases (never the genitive). The accusative form of words is the same as the nominative, outside of a few pronouns.

Unlike High German, Swiss German neuter articles and possessives sometimes feature an "s" on the end, like High German adjectives sometimes do.

The definite articles in the nominative and accusative look like abbreviated versions of the High German nominative ones:

  • de Maa
  • d Frau
  • s Chind
  • d Chind
The indefinite articles in the nominative differ more.
  • en Maa
  • e Frau
  • es Chind
  • Chind
As in High German, you add a "k" (but pronounced as a hard "ch") to the front to negate these. Here the pronunciation also changes back to something closer to High German.
  • kain Maa
  • kai Frau
  • kais Chind
  • kai Chind
The definite articles in the dative also look like the High German ones, with either the first or last letter chopped off
  • mit em Maa
  • mit de Frau
  • mit em Chind
  • mit de Chind
The indefinite, dative articles
  • mit emene Maa
  • mit enere Frau
  • mit emene Chind
  • mit Chind

New Grammar and Constructions

Swiss German has a few points of grammar and constructions that don't appear in High German.

Inversions

In situations, like questions, where the subject follows the verb, the verb and subject often combine into a single, combined form. This happens often in informal, spoken English, for instance "gimme" or "giv'em" instead of "give me" or "give them", respectively. The inverted form of "du" can just be "du", but is often nothing, so Swiss German is "pro drop" in this sense. The "ich" and especially "mir" (we) forms can sound quite distinct from the two-word inversions you'd expect from High German.

  • Häsch Hunger?
  • Hatr Hunge?

  • Wämmer go skifahre?

go

When coming or going in order to do something (a verb), add "go" before the verb. This can look superficially like English sentences like "I'm going to go work", but this comparison is more confusing than helpful.
  • Ich gang go schaffe.
  • Ich chume go schlafe.

Relative pronouns

You use "wo" for all relative pronouns in Swiss German.
  • Ich bi de Maa, wo da wont.

Tuä + verb

One can use "tuä" as a (virtually meaningless?) auxiliary verb.
  • Tuänd Si tschuute?
  • Ich tuä gärn tanze.

n-liasons

We noted that Swiss German tends to drop "n"s, compared to High German. This leaves a lot of vowels at the ends of words, so often an "n" will be introduced to make things flow better.
  • Guete-n-aabig.
  • Min Name-n-isch Tobler.

Verbs

The standard conjugation only has four forms. All the plural forms are the same!
  • ich mache
  • du machsch
  • er macht
  • mir mached
  • ich ha gmacht
Swiss German only uses the compound past tense - there is no preterit (not even with "see" as in "ich war"). Interestingly "gsii" is more regular than the High German "gewesen".
  • ich bin gsi
Past participles work fairly similarly to High German, except that verbs starting with g-, k- ,ch-, b- ,p-, d- ,t-, or z- don't have the "g-" prefix you might expect in the past particple.
  • ich bin cho
  • ich bin gange

    • "du" forms where the stem ends in "s" often add an extra "i" to flow better.

    • du haissisch
    • du issisch
    • du lissisch
