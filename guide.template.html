The goal: understand as much Zurich Swiss German (one of many varieties) as possible with a focused set of N examples.

These are intended to highlight the important differences from High German, purely by example. You'll see longer examples than in typical word lists or sets of flashcards, to resolve ambiguity. Most entries will include both English and High German translations.

This assumes you understand High German at least as well as you hope to understand Swiss German. You can guess much of Swiss German based on High German. Most non-Swiss people make the reasonable choice to learn High German first; it's the written and official language for Swiss German speakers, and there are formalized systems to learn it and be certified (as most immigrants must do to gain residence permits). This isn't to imply High German is higher status or better in daily Swiss life, though: personally, now that I've passed my High German tests, I would love to magically trade all my High German knowledge for Swiss German knowledge.

You should also have some way to listen to Swiss German. It's a spoken language, so this text-only format is severely lacking on its own. (TODO maybe we'll even try to get some audio clips..) Hopefully it helps to have a foreigner try to describe the language from experience trying to learn it, but you also need to be around people speaking it and to be able to connect it to events in your real life. A more ambitious version of this project would include recordings of all terms.

This document tells a story as motivation for all the flashcards, and you can find the set of all flashcards here (TODO).

Entries focus first on examples of common ways to map Swiss German to High German, then moves on to commonly used grammar and vocabulary which differ greatly from High German. The scope is roughly "A1".

Mapping High German to Swiss German

There are several common patterns between corresponding words in High German and Swiss German. Not all of these appear well in writing. "N"s at the end of words in High German commonly disappear.
  • scho
  • vo
  • de Maa
In some cases this isn't even consistently done, even within Zurich Swiss German, particularly with some first-person verb conjugations: TODO: make sure these show both variants somehow
  • ich bi(n)
  • ich cha(n)
You often also lose "ch".
  • au
  • no
  • oi
  • dur (dura?)
  • Züri
  • äntli
Other letters can drop off the end as well
  • grad
  • jetz
And prefixes often lose an "e"
  • grad
  • graad ? (TODO)
You will also notice a lot more hard "ch" sounds (Like Scottish "loch"). When "ch" or "k" appears in a High German word, the corresponding Swiss German word almost always has a hard "ch", though it's often still written as it is in High German.
  • kai
  • choche, koche
If you do want a "k" sound as in English, this becomes "gg" when written
  • brugg
  • zrugg
"st" often becomes "scht"
  • geschter
  • du bisch
  • er isch
  • du hasch
  • de Schtärn
"sp" similarly often becomes "schp"
  • schpile
  • de Gascht
"b" and "p" are closer together, as are "t" and "d", so often these change relative to High German, when written. In general, these are unaspirated.
  • de Puure
  • Dütsch
  • dörfe
Vowels often shift: "i" often becomes "ie", with two vowels sounds (a dipthong)
  • d Liecht
Many words already written with "ie" in High German are also pronounced this way
  • wie
  • de Spiegel
Sometimes "ie" becomes "üü", though!
  • tüüf
"e" often becomes "ä" (which doesn't sound exactly like High German "ä")
  • wie gäht's?
  • schlächt
"ä" can sound different
  • de Chäfer
"i" can become "u"
  • d Brule
"u" often becomes the two-syllable "uä" or "ue"
  • s Buäch
  • guet
"au" often becomes "uu" (or "üü")
  • s Muus
"eu" often becomes "üü"
  • d Lüüt
  • hüüt
"ei" often becomes "ii"
  • sii
  • d Ziit
  • bi
"o" can become "u"
  • d Wuche
  • d Sunne
The diminuitives "-lein" or "-chen" usually become "-li".
  • s Muusli
The "-ung" ending often becomes "-ig". As in High German, "g" at the end of the word sounds more like "k" in English.
  • d Quittig (?)
  • d Rächnig
Many common prepositions change in a predictable way
  • uf
  • uus
  • ai
  • aa
Those also apply when used as verb prefixes. Other prefixes are commonly shortened by dropping the vowel
  • zrugg
  • bsuächt
  • gmacht
Place and language names also change in similar ways
  • Züri
  • Schtai am Rhii
  • Züritüütsch

Articles

Swiss German uses the same three genders as High German, and the nominative, accusative, and dative cases (never the genitive). The accusative form of words is the same as the nominative, outside of a few pronouns. Unlike High German, Swiss German neuter articles and possessives sometimes feature an "s" on the end, like High German adjectives sometimes do. The definite articles in the nominative and accusative look like abbreviated versions of the High German nominative ones:
  • de
  • d
  • s
  • d
The indefinite articles in the nominative differ more
  • en
  • e
  • es
  • -
As in High German, you add a "k" (but pronounced as a hard "ch") to the front to negate these. but here the pronunciation also changes:
  • kain
  • kai
  • kais
  • kai
The definite articles in the dative also look like the High German ones, with either the first or last letter chopped off
  • em
  • de
  • em
  • de
The indefinite, dative articles
  • emene
  • enere
  • emene
  • -

Adjective endings

As in High German, there are quite a few adjective ending rules to learn. However, there is no distinction between the nominative and accusative, the dative is almost uniform, and there is no genitive! Some forms can either be nothing or "-i".

  • di gross(i) Frau - the big woman
  • s alte Huus - the old house
  • di grosse Chind - the big children

      New Grammar and Constructions

      Swiss German has a few points of grammar and constructions that don't appear in High German.

      Inversions and pro-drops

      [Can use picture of pro-drop for du] The inverted form of "du" can just be "du", but is often nothing, so Swiss German is "pro drop" in this sense
      • Hasch Hunger?
      • Hasch du Hunger?
      In the accusative, with reflexive verbs, you have different inversions, including one for "dich"
      • Ich froimi seer
      • (something iwth -di)

      go

      When coming or going in order to do something (a verb), add "go" before the verb. This can look superficially like English sentences like "I'm going to go work", but this comparison is more confusing than helpful.
      • Ich gang go schaffe : I'm going (somewhere) to work
      • Ich chume go schlafe : I'm coming to sleep

      Relative pronouns

      You use "wo" for all relative pronouns in Swiss German.
      • Ich bi de Maa, wo da wont

      Demonstrative Pronouns

      • dies (??)

      Tuä + verb

      One can use "tuä" as a (virtually meaningless?) auxiliary verb.
      • Tuänd Si tschuute?
      • Ich tuä gärn tanze

      n-liasons

      We noted that Swiss German tends to drop "n"s, compared to High German. This leaves a lot of vowels at the ends of words, so often an "n" will be introduced to make things flow better.
      • guete-n-aabig
      • Min Name-n-isch
